Popularity and Subversion

Recently, John Lydon (good thing he doesn’t go by Rotten anymore because he doesn’t deserve it) said that being conservative was the new punk because punk was always the opposite of what was the going concensus or ‘what was popular’ and sense ‘woke’ is popular, the punk thing to do is to oppose that. This is an astonishingly moronic assertion from a guy that I assumed got it. Didn’t he have to get it? Wasn’t that the point? And not just with the Sex Pistols, but on to PIL and beyond? This guy got it, right? I’ve seen this before - in highschool. I came of age in the 80’s and 90’s and gravitated toward the punk subculture - whatever that may mean - once I got to highschool. Before the grunge scene broke, the alternative scene was incredibly heterogenous. You saw the same people at a They Might Be Giants show that you saw at a Cure show that you saw at a Rancid show - more or less. I have written extensively on this elsewhere. The alternative scene was perfectly by Perry Ferrell’s first Lollapalooza. That lineup defined the mentality of all the little subculture niches of alternative on one stage: it was about being genuine and not being merely a commodity. It was all DIY and it was fringe. As a result, it was easy to differentiate ourselves from the Soc's: they listened to pop music and shopped at Dillards; we listened to community radio shows on certain days at certain times and we shopped at Crash-n-Burn. When all the events of the mid-nineties combined and the results became apparent - Ministry stickers on Jocks' pickup trucks, Doc Martins at Dillards, Green Day on the top 40 - we had to intellectualize what we were about without the popularity heuristic. And we did. Well, a lot of us did. A lot of us grew up but some of us committed to the bit. We abhorred the sell-out in principle, but we understood that it had become a monster that artists had no control over. Formerly, we had been our own gatekeepers. We made mixes for our square friends that introduced them to some cool shit but we chose those people and gave them a chance to have a little taste of what we were doing. Sometimes they wanted to shift gears and change their scene; most just thought it gave them a little street cred to know a few things about x, y, or z and that was fine too. Now there was no gate to be kept. Record companies want to sell records, they kind of have to. That doesn't justify the gross exploitation of artists but it does explain why we couldn't count on them to hold the line. And the bands have no control once they've signed off and if you're in a band and someone wants to make it so you can be a full time musician and not washing dishes at Perkins, you're gonna take it unless you really have some spine. Ani Difranco held her ground and Righteous Babe survives untouched. Most of the other labels that our DIY heroes started got subsumed just like the music festivals. WHen I was young, I worked at a law firm in Clearwater, FL. They were a local business started by a handful of young lawyers in the late 70’s and did very well for themselves. Clearwater has a unique, if not well known, distinction in being host to the world headquarters of the Church of Scientology. To an outside it would seem weird to harbor a true disdain for Scientologists, even though their behavior has come undo scrutiny publicly in the last decade or so. However, if you live there, they are seen as a plague. And that was before they started acquiring huge swaths of property. That in and of itself wouldn’t be a big deal but their status as a Church allows them to not pay property taxes. This has decimated the downtown economy - not for the rich, of course, but for the middle class and the “walking around” folks. The own an estimated $600M in property in the downtown corridor and beyond - including several multi-family units for housing their members. That’s somewhere between $8-$12 million in tax revenue that the city is missing out on. Thats a whole other story but I bring it up because there was a long-standing directive at the firm that they weren’t going to do business with the Scientologists. In the 80’s, it hadn’t yet become so predatory a real estate model, but it was a point of contention socially but in the long run it was not an unreasonable policy and it wasn’t any kind of religious bigotry, it was just good business because old Clearwater wasn’t on board. ...

The story we need

- Times like this, we need stories. Personally, I like a nice heartwarming story about a kid and a horse. Or when kids get in over their heads and take on a little more than we think they’re capable of. Those are nice stories. They make me feel good. But feeling good doesn’t get you out of trouble. More often than not, it let’s you ignore the trouble until it’s knocking on your door. Even then, it can be tempting to hold your breath and mute the tv and duck under the covers - crouch and watch for shadows to stop moving under the door. Times like this we need a story about a person that’s just trying to keep his head down and be a worker among workers. You see this guy and he seems a little meek, but you’ve seen stories like this and your gut tells you that he’s just trying not to do something that’ll have consequences beyond his control. This guy has a past he isn’t all that comfortable with but, more than likely, afforded him a certain set of charater traits and skills that have a certain value in difficult circumstances. Woke

These terms seem to be used interchangeably quite often when they, in fact, have an inverse relationship. Liberty is your God-give right to self-actualize, to walk the Earth in search of adventure and enlightenment. If that’s not what you’re doing with it, I can’t help you. Freedom, on the other hand, is a gift from your fellow travelers that helps you maintain your liberty. Often this other is the state but in the long run and at it’s base it is a the gift we give each other whether it is part of a social contract to be a civilized, well-ordered people, whether it is in my self-interest to reciprocate such in principle, or whether I am simply indifferent to you. God gave you the tools to exploit the successes of those who reared and educated you and to recover from their mistakes. God gave you the ability to think critically and to conduct yourself in manner that will encourage your success whether it is as a member of that civilized, well-ordered group, whether you realize your success is contingent upon others’, or whether it’s by minding your own business. For those already hung up on the use of God so far, it is merely a shorthand that reflects the attitude of the author. The words above survive the replacement of God with whatever source of life or consciousness you ascribe to. Ulitmately, Liberty is innate to our being. A Phenomenological Exploration of the Ontological Effects of Alcohol and Drug Addiction

A Phenomenological Exploration of the Ontological Effects of Alcohol and Drug Addiction The intention of this paper is to introduce the idea that experiencing an alcoholic bottom is akin to a trauma of a kind and may, therefore, be the basis for cases of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). There are many cases wherein drug and/or alcohol abuse or addiction co-occurs with an instance of PTSD, but it is concluded or assumed that they are separate matters or, more often, that the PTSD has exacerbated a reaction in the form of addiction to cope with the symptoms. I would like to suggest that the existential trauma experienced by the suffering alcoholic may be more pronounced than a mere state of being. In “A Phenomenology of Addiction,” David Lenson attempts to provide a phenomenological account of the nature of addiction. While his insights regarding temporality are interesting and will be explored in this paper, his focus on the mechanism of addiction, while topical, is not phenomenological insofar as it ignores the lived experience of the addict. In his chapter, Lenson (1995) proposes that “Unless drinking comes to engulf all hours of the day. . . the division of the day into drinking and sober hours provides a relativity for time atomized by addiction. When one drinks all the time, that relativity is lost, and the drinker is ‘bottoming out.’ It is at this stage that drinkers either die or seek help” (p.44). This is an oversimplification if not entirely inaccurate. In fact, the focus of this paper is that the very stage of ‘bottoming out’ is not so temporally distinct and an immediate vector towards death or recovery is rarely quickly determined. Once the relativity of time is lost, the alcoholic is in a state of suspension that can last years or decades. In this state one experiences the “existential vacuum” of which Victor Frankl speaks in his memoir of the death camps (2019, p.105).
