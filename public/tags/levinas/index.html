A Phenomenological Exploration of the Ontological Effects of Alcohol and Drug Addiction

A Phenomenological Exploration of the Ontological Effects of Alcohol and Drug Addiction The intention of this paper is to introduce the idea that experiencing an alcoholic bottom is akin to a trauma of a kind and may, therefore, be the basis for cases of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). There are many cases wherein drug and/or alcohol abuse or addiction co-occurs with an instance of PTSD, but it is concluded or assumed that they are separate matters or, more often, that the PTSD has exacerbated a reaction in the form of addiction to cope with the symptoms. I would like to suggest that the existential trauma experienced by the suffering alcoholic may be more pronounced than a mere state of being. In “A Phenomenology of Addiction,” David Lenson attempts to provide a phenomenological account of the nature of addiction. While his insights regarding temporality are interesting and will be explored in this paper, his focus on the mechanism of addiction, while topical, is not phenomenological insofar as it ignores the lived experience of the addict. In his chapter, Lenson (1995) proposes that “Unless drinking comes to engulf all hours of the day. . . the division of the day into drinking and sober hours provides a relativity for time atomized by addiction. When one drinks all the time, that relativity is lost, and the drinker is ‘bottoming out.’ It is at this stage that drinkers either die or seek help” (p.44). This is an oversimplification if not entirely inaccurate. In fact, the focus of this paper is that the very stage of ‘bottoming out’ is not so temporally distinct and an immediate vector towards death or recovery is rarely quickly determined. Once the relativity of time is lost, the alcoholic is in a state of suspension that can last years or decades. In this state one experiences the “existential vacuum” of which Victor Frankl speaks in his memoir of the death camps (2019, p.105). As I am not a psychiatrist, I cannot speak to an actual diagnosis of PTSD but I am able to assess and regurgitate the diagnostic criteria set forth in the discipline’s standard diagnostic text – the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-V).
