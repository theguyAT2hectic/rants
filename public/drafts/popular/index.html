Popularity and Subversion

April 29, 2026 · 5 min · J. McMullen

Recently, John Lydon (good thing he doesn’t go by Rotten anymore because he doesn’t deserve it) said that being conservative was the new punk because punk was always the opposite of what was the going concensus or ‘what was popular’ and sense ‘woke’ is popular, the punk thing to do is to oppose that. This is an astonishingly moronic assertion from a guy that I assumed got it. Didn’t he have to get it? Wasn’t that the point? And not just with the Sex Pistols, but on to PIL and beyond? This guy got it, right?#


I’ve seen this before - in highschool. I came of age in the 80’s and 90’s and gravitated toward the punk subculture - whatever that may mean - once I got to highschool. Before the grunge scene broke, the alternative scene was incredibly heterogenous. You saw the same people at a They Might Be Giants show that you saw at a Cure show that you saw at a Rancid show - more or less. I have written extensively on this elsewhere. The alternative scene was perfectly by Perry Ferrell’s first Lollapalooza. That lineup defined the mentality of all the little subculture niches of alternative on one stage: it was about being genuine and not being merely a commodity. It was all DIY and it was fringe.#


As a result, it was easy to differentiate ourselves from the Soc’s: they listened to pop music and shopped at Dillards; we listened to community radio shows on certain days at certain times and we shopped at Crash-n-Burn. When all the events of the mid-nineties combined and the results became apparent - Ministry stickers on Jocks’ pickup trucks, Doc Martins at Dillards, Green Day on the top 40 - we had to intellectualize what we were about without the popularity heuristic. And we did. Well, a lot of us did. A lot of us grew up but some of us committed to the bit. We abhorred the sell-out in principle, but we understood that it had become a monster that artists had no control over. Formerly, we had been our own gatekeepers. We made mixes for our square friends that introduced them to some cool shit but we chose those people and gave them a chance to have a little taste of what we were doing. Sometimes they wanted to shift gears and change their scene; most just thought it gave them a little street cred to know a few things about x, y, or z and that was fine too. Now there was no gate to be kept. Record companies want to sell records, they kind of have to. That doesn’t justify the gross exploitation of artists but it does explain why we couldn’t count on them to hold the line. And the bands have no control once they’ve signed off and if you’re in a band and someone wants to make it so you can be a full time musician and not washing dishes at Perkins, you’re gonna take it unless you really have some spine. Ani Difranco held her ground and Righteous Babe survives untouched. Most of the other labels that our DIY heroes started got subsumed just like the music festivals.

WHen I was young, I worked at a law firm in Clearwater, FL. They were a local business started by a handful of young lawyers in the late 70’s and did very well for themselves. Clearwater has a unique, if not well known, distinction in being host to the world headquarters of the Church of Scientology. To an outside it would seem weird to harbor a true disdain for Scientologists, even though their behavior has come undo scrutiny publicly in the last decade or so. However, if you live there, they are seen as a plague. And that was before they started acquiring huge swaths of property. That in and of itself wouldn’t be a big deal but their status as a Church allows them to not pay property taxes. This has decimated the downtown economy - not for the rich, of course, but for the middle class and the “walking around” folks. The own an estimated $600M in property in the downtown corridor and beyond - including several multi-family units for housing their members. That’s somewhere between $8-$12 million in tax revenue that the city is missing out on. Thats a whole other story but I bring it up because there was a long-standing directive at the firm that they weren’t going to do business with the Scientologists. In the 80’s, it hadn’t yet become so predatory a real estate model, but it was a point of contention socially but in the long run it was not an unreasonable policy and it wasn’t any kind of religious bigotry, it was just good business because old Clearwater wasn’t on board.

The Scientologists do business under a lot of names and LLCs and often their members buy residential properties and donate them to the Church. This can make it difficult to avoid doing business with them accidentally. I wish that were the case with Johnson, Pope, but one of the real estate partners ended up sneaking a client in on a lucrative matter. Then the floodgates opened. They paid and they paid well so the lion got through the door. Almost instantly the litigation department was involved in whatever douchebaggery was needed to help alleviate the pressure after the Church tortured and killed Lisa McPherson.

Perry Ferrell started Lollapalooza in 1991. The lineup: Jane’s Addiction, Siouxsie and the Banshees, Lving Colour, Nine Inch Nails, Body Count (Ice-T’s hardcore project), the Rollins Band, Butthole Surfers, Violent Femmes and Fishbone.
